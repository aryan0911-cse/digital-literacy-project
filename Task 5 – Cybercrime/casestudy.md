Unified Payments Interface (UPI) fraud involves the use of deceptive tactics to trick users into authorizing unauthorized digital fund transfers. Unlike traditional hacking, these scams primarily use social engineering—manipulating the victim's trust rather than breaking into the banking system itself.How it Happens,A common scenario, often reported on marketplaces like OLX, follows these steps:The Hook: A scammer poses as a buyer for an item you listed online. They agree to your price immediately without haggling to build excitement.The Deception: The "buyer" claims they are sending the payment via UPI but sends a QR code or a "Collect Request" instead of a direct transfer.The Action: They tell the victim, "Scan this code to receive your payment."The Theft: When the victim scans the code and enters their UPI PIN, they are actually authorizing a debit. Instead of receiving money, the funds are instantly pulled from their account.Target Audience and ConsequencesWhile anyone can be a target, scammers frequently profile students and young professionals (who trade frequently online) and senior citizens (who may be less familiar with UPI mechanics).The consequences are often devastating. Beyond the immediate financial loss, which can range from a few thousand to several lakhs of rupees, victims face significant emotional distress and a loss of trust in digital systems. Recovery is difficult because UPI transfers are instantaneous and "push-based," meaning once a user enters their PIN, the bank considers the transaction "authorized," making reversals nearly impossible unless reported within minutes to the 1930 cyber-helpline.
